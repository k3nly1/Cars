The Diesel Engine & Its Legacy

Diesel engines have powered heavy transport, industry, and many passenger cars for more than a century. They are known for their durability, efficiency, and strong low-end torque.

Origins of Diesel Power

In 1893, Rudolf Diesel patented the first compression-ignition engine that would later bear his name. Diesel engines became popular for ships, trains, and trucks due to their ability to run on heavier fuels and deliver high torque.

Unlike gasoline engines, diesel engines ignite fuel through high compression rather than spark plugs. This makes them more fuel-efficient, but also means they require robust components and emissions control systems.

Key Phases in Diesel Car Development

  • 1890s: First diesel engines invented and used in industrial applications
  • 1930s-1950s: Diesel trucks and buses become common
  • 1970s: Diesel passenger cars gain popularity in Europe during fuel crises
  • 1990s: Turbocharging and electronic injection improve performance and efficiency
  • 2000s: Emissions regulations drive adoption of diesel particulate filters and SCR systems
  • 2010s: Dieselgate prompts stricter testing and a shift toward alternative fuels

Modern Diesel Engine Features

  • Fuel Efficiency
    • 20-30% better fuel economy than gasoline engines
      • Urban driving: 5-6 L/100km
      • Highway driving: 3-4 L/100km
    • Lower CO2 emissions per km
  • Engine Performance
    • High torque delivery at low RPM
      • 300-500 Nm torque available from 1500 RPM
      • Excellent for towing and hauling
    • Durable engine design lasting 300,000+ km

Diesel Milestones

  1. 1893 - Rudolf Diesel patents the diesel engine
  2. 1936 - First diesel passenger car (Mercedes-Benz 260 D)
  3. 1973 - Oil crisis drives diesel car demand in Europe
  4. 1993 - Common rail injection introduced in passenger cars
  5. 2000 - Diesel particulate filters become standard in many markets
  6. 2015 - Emissions scandal leads to stricter diesel testing worldwide

Diesel Engine Terms

Compression-Ignition
Fuel ignites due to high compression and heat within the cylinder, not a spark plug.
Turbocharging
Boosts air intake to increase power and efficiency in diesel engines.
Diesel Particulate Filter (DPF)
Catches soot particles to reduce emissions and meet environmental regulations.
Selective Catalytic Reduction (SCR)
Uses urea-based fluid to reduce NOx emissions in modern diesel cars.

Diesel vs Gasoline

Characteristic Diesel Gasoline
Fuel Efficiency Higher (more km per liter) Lower
Torque High at low RPM Lower at low RPM
Emissions Lower CO2 but higher particulates/NOx Higher CO2 but lower particulates
Maintenance More expensive (DPF, SCR) Generally cheaper
